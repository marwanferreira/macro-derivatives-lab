What went wrong during entry:
The main issue was not the market thesis but the platform’s margin handling. The original intention was to put on 7 spreads, but the simulator rejected the short 108 Call leg for size 7, then also rejected size 6 and size 5 in one path, before a later clean 5-lot sequence was successfully entered after reducing the long-call position. This showed that the simulator does not always margin option spreads cleanly when entered leg by leg at larger size.

Why the trade was resized:
The trade was originally meant to be larger in order to generate meaningful PnL, but the platform forced a reduction because the short call leg could not be entered at the intended size under the simulator margin rules. Resizing the structure to 5 spreads was the cleanest way to restore the intended bull call spread profile while avoiding an excessively large naked long-call exposure.

Main lesson from this trade so far:
The execution problem here was not slippage at exit, but rather platform implementation and margin recognition during entry. Even when the economic structure is a defined-risk spread, the simulator may reject the short option leg if the position is built leg by leg at too large a size. In practice, this means execution size must be adapted to the simulator’s margin mechanics, not just to the theoretical risk of the strategy.
